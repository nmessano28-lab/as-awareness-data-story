Personal note

I’m Nate, and I live with ankylosing spondylitis.

When I was 20 years old, I woke up one morning feeling like I had tweaked my neck. But the pain never stopped. Over time, it spread down my spine and became part of my daily life.

For years, I tried to find answers through primary care, chiropractors, orthopedists, and physical therapy, but nothing fully explained what was happening. It was not until someone close to me suggested that I might have ankylosing spondylitis that I learned about rheumatology and finally found a path toward relief.

Not knowing that rheumatology was even an option delayed my understanding of my own condition. This project uses data visualization and public health research to make ankylosing spondylitis easier to recognize, explain, and understand.

Medical disclaimer: This project is for public awareness and data storytelling only. It is not medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment guidance. Anyone with persistent pain, stiffness, eye symptoms, or health concerns should speak with a qualified healthcare professional.

Why awareness matters

A condition that can look ordinary at first

Ankylosing spondylitis can begin as back or hip pain and stiffness, but public sources describe it as an inflammatory disease that may affect the spine, joints, eyes, gut, skin, mobility, and fatigue.

Age of onset

Young people can be living with an old-sounding disease.

Back pain in young adulthood is often treated like a strain or posture issue. For AS, that assumption can make the earliest clues easier to miss.

The diagnosis gap

Many people do not go straight from symptoms to rheumatology.

AS can be hard to recognize because pain is common, imaging may not show early changes, and no single test confirms the condition. This creates a path where people may collect appointments before they collect answers.

Project method

What this project demonstrates

This project combines health communication, source synthesis, structured data, interaction design, and visual storytelling. The goal is not to prove a clinical claim. The goal is to translate reputable public information into a clearer public awareness experience.

01

Research synthesis

Gathered public health explanations and converted them into a source-backed story structure.

02

Structured data

Created small datasets for facts, symptoms, diagnosis journey stages, and invisibility comparisons.

03

Data visualization

Used interactive visuals to make onset, diagnostic delay, and daily impact easier to understand.
